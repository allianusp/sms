Paper Abstract.  Software variability has been widely used for more than 25 years to describe the variability of software systems. Because software architectures are the cornerstone of the design process, variability concerns are often described in the architecture to represent the common and variable aspects of systems. As different modeling techniques and languages are available, we provide in this chapter an overview and current trends on the existing alternatives for modeling software variability in architecture and this overview could serve as a guide for researchers and practitioners to choose modeling alternatives.


Variability in Software Architecture: A Systematic Mapping Study

We present here the complete SMS protocol, together with the planning, execution, and reporting results.

Planning

The research method adopted in this study follows the guidelines proposed by Kitchenham and Charters (2007) and Petersen et al. (2015). Our SMS is expected to provide a broad overview about how to identify, analyze, synthesize, evaluate, realize, and derive architecture with variability. This SMS is carried out according to a predefined search strategy, which begins with the definition of a protocol to specify the research questions to be addressed and the method that will be used to perform the analysis.

Research Questions

  • RQ1: How has variability been identified in the software architecture?
  • RQ2: How has variability in software architecture been represented?
  • RQ2.1: How the binding time property can be represented in the software architecture?
  • RQ3: How software architectures that contain variability information have been evaluated?
  • RQ4: How derivation techniques are used to configure the variability in the architecture?
  • RQ5: How do tools support the maintenance and evolution of variability in software architecture?

Selection Criteria

Inclusion Criteria:

  • IC1. The primary study addresses variability in software architectures

Exclusion Criteria:

  • E1: If two studies address the same topic, the less complete one is excluded
  • E2: Short study below four pages
  • E3: Duplicated studies
  • E4: Study containing an editorial, abstract, or introduction
  • E5: Study not written in English

Quality Assessment Checklist:

  • QA1. Is there a clear motivation to conduct the research?
  • QA2. Is there a proper explanation of the context (industry or academia) in which the research was performed?
  • QA3. Was the research proper designed to address the goals of the research?
  • QA4. Does the study include enough detail about the variability approach in the architecture level?
  • QA5. Has the variability approach presented in the study been empirically evaluated?
  • QA6. Did the study present a review of previous research about the topic?
  • QA7. Do the findings of the study provide adequate data to support any conclusions?
  • QA8. Did the study present further research data?
